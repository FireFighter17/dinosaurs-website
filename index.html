What were dinosaurs?

Dinosaurs were a diverse group of reptiles that lived during the Mesozoic Era — roughly 252 to 66 million years ago. The Mesozoic is traditionally divided into three periods:

  • Triassic — 252–201 million years ago (Ma)
  • Jurassic — 201–145 Ma
  • Cretaceous — 145–66 Ma

They included small feathered species and massive long-necked giants. Birds are modern descendants of one group of theropod dinosaurs.

Mesozoic Timeline (quick)

Triassic (252–201 Ma)

Dinosaurs first appear; ecosystems recover after the Permian extinction.

Jurassic (201–145 Ma)

Large dinosaurs like sauropods and many theropods diversify; first birds appear.

Cretaceous (145–66 Ma)

Flowering plants spread; iconic species such as Tyrannosaurus and Triceratops lived then. Ended with a mass extinction.

Main dinosaur groups

Theropods

Mostly bipedal carnivores — e.g., Tyrannosaurus, Velociraptor. Birds evolved from small theropods.

Sauropods

Giant long-necked herbivores — e.g., Brachiosaurus, Apatosaurus.

Ornithischians

Herbivores with diverse armor and horns — e.g., Triceratops, Stegosaurus.

Tyrannosaurus rex

One of the largest theropods; lived late Cretaceous (~68–66 Ma).

Triceratops

Large horned ornithischian from the late Cretaceous.

Velociraptor

Small, feathered predator from the late Cretaceous (Mongolia).

Brachiosaurus

Large sauropod notable for its long forelimbs and tall neck.

Allosaurus

Allosaurus was a large meat-eating dinosaur that lived in prehistoric North America from the late Jurassic.

Ceratosaurus

One of the medium size theropods from the late Jurassic.

Stegosaurus

A large, slow moving plant-eater, defending it self with its powerful spiked tail, lived late Jurassic.

Ankylosaurus

A walking herbivore tank from the late Cretaceous.

Deinocheirus

Deinocheirusus was a small Theropod. It lived in what is now North America during the early Cretaceous period.

Sarcosuchus

An extinct genus of crocodyliform that lived during the Early Cretaceous.

Quetzalcoatlus

Classified in the family Azhdarchidae, which contains some of the largest known flying animals, lived during the Cretaceous period.

Pterodactyl

Pterodactyls were flying reptiles of prehistoric times. They lived about 145 to 65.5 million years ago.

Spinosaurus

Spinosaurus was a large theropod from the late Cretaceous. Scientists think it may have lived a semi-aquatic lifestyle.

Giganotosaurus

Giganotosaurus was a very large theropod that lived in prehistoric South America from the Cretaceous.

Titanosaurus

The biggest sauropod that walked on earth, which lived from the Late Jurassic Epoch (163.5 million to 145 million years ago) to the end of the Cretaceous Period(145 million to 66 million years ago).

Therizinosaurus

From the late Cretaceous. Therizinosaurus was a large plant-eating dinosaur that walked on two legs. It had enormous claws on each of its three-fingered hands. These claws are thought to be the longest of any land animal ever.

Concavenator

Theropod from the Cretaceous epoch, with a strange hump on its back. It has bird-like appendages on its forearms.

Amargasaurus

Saurepod from the early Cretatious, had a double row of spines along its back which may have supported a twin 'sail' of skin.

Diplodocus

From the late Jurassic, Diplodocus had a long neck that it would have used to reach high and low vegetation, and to drink water.

Ceolophysis

One of the smallest theropods of the late Jurassic, it sharp teeth and grasping claws helped to catch the food.

Protoceratops

A ceratopsian with thenth of 1.8 merers; from the late Cretacious.

Proceratosaurus

Small theropod from the mid Jurassic.

Plateosaurus

Prosauropod that had 5-fingered hands and a large thumb claw, which may have been for defence or for collecting food. From the late Triassic period

dilophosaurus

Dilophosaurus was a large theropod that lived in what's now North America. From the early Jurassic.

Gigantoraptor

Gigantoraptor is one of the largest omnivorous dinosaurs. Lived late Cretaceous.

Carcharodontosaurus

Large theropod from the late Cretaceous. Carcharodontosaurus teeth look a lot like shark teeth. Like Tyrannosaurus, it had about 60 of them.

Iguanodon

From the early Cretaceous, Iguanodon is a large ornithischian. Iguanodon could probably walk on all fours or on two legs. It had a large thumb spike on the end of its hand, probably to fend-off predators.

Ornithomimus

Ornithomimus was a swift, bipedal dinosaur which was covered in feathers and equipped with a small toothless beak that may indicate an omnivorous diet. Lived late Cretaceous.

Fun facts

  • Some dinosaurs had feathers — not all were scaly.
  • Birds are technically modern dinosaurs.
  • Fossils form when organic material is replaced by minerals over long periods.
  • The asteroid impact at ~66 Ma at Chicxulub (Yucatán) is the leading explanation for the mass extinction.
